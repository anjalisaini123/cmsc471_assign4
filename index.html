What Influences the Length of Atlantic Storms?

The hurricanes and tropical storms of the Atlantic have varying lengths and occur at different times of the year. We wanted to find which factors from our data set impact the length of an Atlantic storm.

Exploration of our data set led us to find that the maximum wind speed of the storm, the diameter of the storm, and the month in which the storm occurred all have some influence over the length of Atlantic storms. The following visulizations will help to show their influence.

This visulization shows the relationship between the wind speed and the length of the storms. (more info will be added later)

The diameter of an area experiencing over 34 knots of wind and the length of the storm has a positive correlation. So, it seems that storms which affect a larger area tend to last longer.

Hover over a dot to see which storm it is!

The diameter of an area experiencing over 64 knots of wind and the length of the storm also has a generally positive correlation, but not nearly as strong as the one above!

This is likely because higher speed winds are harder to sustain for long periods of time. So, areas with over 64 knots of wind tend to not last as long as areas with over 34 knots of wind.

There is one outlier, hurricane Nadine in 2012. Nadine was an erratic category 1 hurricane. So, it had lower wind speeds compared to other hurricanes. However, Nadine was erratic in the sense that it continued to oscillate between weakening and strengthing it's wind speed based on it's location. This resulted in Nadine to become the 4th longest lived hurricane in the Atlantic ocean.

Note that many storms do not even reach over 64 knots of wind speed. So, there respective diameters are 0.

Overall, we still see that larger storms tend to last longer.

Here we have the relationship between the month in which the storms occur and their average duration in hours.

Many storms occur in August and September, which lie near the end of Summer. Tropical storms form in warm, low-pressure areas over the ocean. Because the ocean waters have been warmed throughout the Summer season, the end-of-Summer months tend to be the most conducive for many strong, lengthy storms.
